Scottish Bat Species

Common Pipistrelle (Pipistrellus pipistrellus) and Soprano Pipistrelle (Pipistrellus pygmaeus) were first described as two seperate species about two decades ago. Their echolocation calls are distint, with a little overlap. These are the most common bat species in Scotland. The Soprano is found widely across central Scotland with the Common less.... common. They are Scotland's smallest bats and can crawl into a roosting space no thicker than your little finger. They can be found in cavity walls (often in mordern buildings), on wall-heads, within flat roofs and espcially in the space between roofing tiles or slates and the timber sarking which lines them in many buildings. Both species are generalists and can be found hunting in gardens, along hedgerows and around woodland edges. Commons are often found in farmland in Scotland. Pipistrelles eat relatively small insects: midges, micro-moths etc., which they hunt by hawking, i.e., catching their prey in flight. Summer maternity colonies of both species are large, but especially the Soprano, which gather in groups of many hundreds or even thousands. Commons tend to breed in groups of up to 100. They often move between summer maternity roosts. Males roost individually or in small groups through the summer and then set up breeding territories in the autumn, centred on a roost. They then display song-flight behaviour, calling to females to join them in harem groups. Hibernation tends to be solitary or in small groups, in cracks or crevices with a suitable microclimate.

Daubenton’s Bat (Myotis daubentonii) or “Daubies” are one of a genus of medium-sized bats, known as Myotis bats, which are easily mistaken for one another in flight, as they have very similar calls. Daubenton’s can sometimes be visually distinguished as they are the only UK species which hunts by gaffing their prey. They fly very low over the surface of a lake or river and catch emerging aquatic insects in their feet. When watched they almost look hovercraft-like. They roost in tree crevices or in stone structures, such as bridges or culverts and have high roost affinity, only rarely changing their roost locations. Breeding colonies can comprise as many as 100 bats. They tend to cluster very closely within these groups, allowing them to be less dependent on warmth for maternity roosts than other bat species. Winter hibernation, in common with other Myotis species, tends to take place underground. They require constant temperature of between 2 and 8 degrees Celsius and high humidity, which they find deep in caves and mines. This is the only bat species in the UK which has been found to carry European Bat Lyssavirus (EBLV2), a form of rabies. Extensive testing has shown that, whilst endemic, the virus or its associated antibodies are present in a very small number of individuals. Nonetheless care should be taken near this species. If handling of a bat is essential, then this should never be done without gloves.

Natterer’s Bat (Myotis nattereri) are primarily woodland specialists, though they also feed low over pasture. They are a slow flying species and are even capable of hovering. This allows them to glean prey (i.e. pick them off vegetation), as well as hawking. Their slow flight creates vulnerability to predators, for which reason they tend to emerge very late after sunset. They eat moths and larger prey caught by hawking, as well as spiders, harvestmen and similar arthropods, which are caught by gleaning. Natterer’s Bats are difficult to tell from other Myotis species, especially from their echolocation calls, which are very similar to those of their co-generics. However, they fly significantly slower than those species and their calls are a little quieter, which can sometimes help with identification. They follow a similar roosting ecology to Daubenton’s Bat, gathering in maternity colonies of up to 80 and hibernating underground. Their summer breeding roosts may be in deep masonry crevices, but also in attics or in tree holes. Unlike Daubenton’s they move roost frequently, often remaining in a roost for only 2-3 nights at a time. Myotis species are thought to find mates by gathering outside hibernation sites in the autumn and swarming. Males remain for many nights whilst females arrive and return to their summer roosts after mating.

Brown Long-eared Bat (Plecotus auritus) are highly specialised woodland specialists. Like the Natterer’s Bat they fly slowly and can hover to glean their prey from vegetation, as well as hawking large insects in flight. Their primary prey is large moths. Some moths, especially Noctuids have rudimentary ears, allowing them to hear the echolocation of an approaching bat and take avoiding action. Long- eared Bats counter this by having especially faint echolocation and using their massive ears to listen for the movements of their prey. Moths are often taken to feeding perches to eat. These locations can be identified by the piles of discarded moth wings below them. They are a hard species to survey, as they emerge when light levels are low, and their faint calls make them difficult to hear with a bat detector more than 5m away. This species usually roosts in large attic spaces, haylofts or in hollow trees, almost exclusively within 100m of deciduous woodland. They have high roost affinity and males are sometimes present within the summer maternity colonies. Hibernation may take place underground (though not usually as deep as Myotis bats) or in cellars etc. They tend to fly extensively within roost spaces, scattering droppings, which often have a distinctive sheen, caused by moth wing scales.

Noctule (Nyctalus noctula) is Scotland's biggest bat species, often twice the size and four times the weight of Pipistrelles. They are fast, strong fliers and have been known to cover 50km in one night. Due to their size and speed of flight they are less vulnerable to predators than other bat species and often emerge before dark, when they can be mistaken for Swifts. They are skilled hawkers, catching large beetles or other flying insects, high above woodland or pasture. They appear to have an affinity with grazed river valleys. In Scotland Noctules are a scarce species, though they are occasionally found throughout Lothians and Borders. They have especially loud echolocation calls, which can sometimes be picked up with a bat detector from several hundred metres away. Noctule colonies usually roost in tree holes, often very high, making roosts of this species difficult to find. In England, where they are more frequent, they are occasionally found roosting in attics. In autumn males set up territories around harem roosts, like Pipistrelles. Instead of song flighting male Noctules call from the roost entrance and, as their social calls are within our hearing range it is sometimes possible to find these roosts by listening for them.

Less Common Species: Three other bat species have been recorded in southern Scotland, but are rarely encountered: Nathusius Pipistrelle (Pipistrellus nathusii) is slightly larger than the other two Pipistrelles. Until recently those encountered were thought to be stray migrants from continental Europe, but we now know they breed in this country. They have a strong affinity to large inland water bodies: the biggest known colonies in the UK are around Lough Neagh in Northern Ireland. Leisler’s Bat (Nyctalus leisleri) is similar to the Noctule, though smaller. It is a woodland specialist and recent research has suggested they may have very small territories. They are found in the south west of Scotland. Whiskered Bat (Myotis mystacinus) is similar to Natterer’s Bat and appears to have an affinity to parkland and farmland. It may be under-recorded as it is a difficult species to identify. Its calls are very similar to those of Daubenton’s Bat and Natterer’s Bat.

In the hand, it is very hard to differentiate from two other Myotis bats: Brandts Bat (Myotis brandtii) and Alcathoe’s Bat (Myotis alcathoe). Brandts bat has been recorded once in Scotland, at a roost in Galloway. These three species are hard to differentiate and are collectively referred to as “Small Myotis”. Alcathoe’s was only identified in the UK for the first time in 2010, so it is not impossible that they may yet be found in Scotland. Various other bat species could turn up in Scotland. The Serotine (Eptesicus serotinus) is a large bat species traditionally found in the south of England. In recent years there have been many records of this species in County Durham, less than 100 miles from the Scottish Border. In 2011 a Parti-coloured Bat (Vespertlio murinus) was found in Arran, in the Western Isles. As this species is normally found in continental Europe, we must assume it was blown there in bad weather.
